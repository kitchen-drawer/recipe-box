Mischiato Potente: Mixed Pasta and Seafood Stew

Yields: 4 servings

This dish, with its roots in cucina povera and its journey to a celebrated restaurant dish, is a testament to how humble ingredients can create something truly spectacular. I just love how it celebrates the idea of varying pasta textures in one harmonious bite. Let's get cooking!

Ingredients

For the Fish Stock (yields about 4 cups):

  • 2 tablespoons olive oil
  • 1 large yellow onion (roughly chopped)
  • 1 large carrot (roughly chopped)
  • 1 celery stalk (roughly chopped)
  • 10 large garlic cloves (peeled)
  • 2 tablespoons double-concentrated tomato paste
  • 2 pounds fish bones (preferably with heads on)
  • 1 cup dry white wine
  • 2 large tomatoes (chopped, or 2 cups canned tomatoes, strained)
  • 1 lemon (cut into quarters)
  • 5 fresh oregano sprigs
  • 3 fresh tarragon sprigs
  • 3 fresh rosemary sprigs
  • 2 quarts cold water (or enough to cover bones by 1 inch)
  • Kosher salt, to taste

For the Mischiato:

  • 2 sea bass fillets (or striped bass, black bass, or branzino; about 6 ounces each), skin on
  • ¼ cup plus 2 tablespoons olive oil
  • 1 teaspoon kosher salt
  • Fresh coarsely ground black pepper
  • 3 cups Fish Stock (prepared above)
  • ¼ pound mixed pastas (such as penne, trofie, orecchiette, fusilli, and spaghetti; if using longer shapes, break them into 2- to 3-inch pieces; or a packaged mix of pasta shapes)
  • 1 cup small red cherry tomatoes (or jarred Italian cherry tomatoes, drained of liquid)
  • ½ lemon
  • 1 tablespoon colatura di alici (Italian fish sauce)
  • 2 tablespoons finely chopped fresh Italian parsley leaves (for garnish)
  • 2 tablespoons finishing-quality extra-virgin olive oil (for garnish)

For the Fennel Salad:

  • 1 large fennel bulb (preferably with the tops; about 1 pound, or 12 ounces for the bulb)
  • ½ lemon
  • ½ teaspoon kosher salt
  • 1 tablespoon finishing-quality extra-virgin olive oil
  • 1 tablespoon finely chopped fresh Italian parsley leaves

Instructions

I. Preparing the Fish Stock (do this first, as it's the foundation of your stew):

  1. Gather Your Aromatics: Get your onion, carrot, celery, and garlic ready. Roughly chop them all, aiming for pieces that are fairly uniform in size so they cook evenly. This is your mirepoix, the aromatic backbone of your stock.
  2. Sauté the Vegetables: In a large stockpot, pour in 2 tablespoons of olive oil. Turn the heat to medium-high and let it warm for about a minute. You want the oil to feel just slightly warm, not smoking. Add your chopped onions, carrot, celery, and garlic to the pot. Cook them for 5 to 6 minutes, stirring occasionally. Don't let them brown; we're looking to sweat them, allowing them to release their moisture and soften without developing any deep color. This gentle cooking will yield a clearer, brighter stock.
  3. Caramelize the Tomato Paste: Once the vegetables are soft, push them to one side of the pot, creating a clear space. Add the 2 tablespoons of double-concentrated tomato paste directly to the hot pot bottom. Stir it constantly for about 2 minutes, allowing it to caramelize. This step is crucial; caramelizing the tomato paste deepens its flavor, adding a layer of rich umami to your stock. Be vigilant here, as you don't want it to burn.
  4. Introduce the Fish Bones and Wine: Now, bring your fish bones to the party! Add them to the pot along with the 1 cup of dry white wine. Increase the heat slightly and cook for 2 to 3 minutes, stirring and scraping the bottom of the pot to release any flavorful bits that might have stuck. The wine will deglaze the pan, picking up all those delicious caramelized fond from the tomato paste and vegetables. Let almost all the wine evaporate.
  5. Add Remaining Ingredients and Simmer: Toss in your chopped large tomatoes, the quartered lemon, and the fresh oregano, tarragon, and rosemary sprigs. Pour in 2 quarts of cold water, or just enough to cover the fish bones by about an inch. Bring the stock to a boil over high heat. Once it hits a rolling boil, reduce the heat to low to maintain a gentle, gentle simmer. You'll see some foam rise to the surface; patiently skim this off with a spoon. This helps keep your stock clear and clean-tasting. Continue to simmer for 1 hour and 20 minutes. Remember, a gentle simmer is key here; you don't want to violently boil your stock, as that can churn impurities back into the liquid and result in a cloudy, less delicate flavor.
  6. Strain and Cool: Turn off the heat and let the stock cool slightly. Place a mesh strainer over a large pot or bowl. Carefully pour the stock through the strainer, pressing firmly on the solids to extract every last drop of flavorful liquid. Discard the solids. You'll have about 4 cups of beautiful, aromatic fish stock. If you're not using it immediately, cool it to room temperature, then cover tightly and refrigerate. It'll keep for a few days, or you can freeze it for longer storage.

II. Preparing the Fennel Salad (do this while the fish stock is simmering):

  1. Trim the Fennel: Take your large fennel bulb. Trim off the green tops at the point where the bulb starts to separate into stalks. (Don't discard these; they're great for stock!) Trim a tiny bit off the very bottom of the root end, making sure to leave enough of the core so the bulb stays intact. Remove any outer layers that look bruised or unappealing. Chop those trimmings and set them aside to add to your fish stock.
  2. Slice the Fennel: Adjust a mandoline to slice about ⅛ inch thick. Cut the fennel bulb in half lengthwise. Lay one half, cut-side down, on the mandoline. Make a couple of test slices to check the thickness. You want the slices to be delicate enough that they don't feel like thick slabs, but sturdy enough that they don't tear or fall apart. Adjust your mandoline blade as needed. Continue slicing the fennel, always being mindful of your fingers as you get closer to the blade. Pop that end piece into your mouth for a little chef's snack, or save it for stock.
  3. Store the Slices: Place the beautiful, translucent fennel slices in a medium bowl. Cover them with a damp paper towel to keep them fresh and crisp, and set them aside until you're ready to serve.

III. Cooking the Mischiato and Sea Bass:

  1. Preheat Your Oven: Adjust an oven rack to the center position. Preheat your oven to 375°F.
  2. Season the Sea Bass: Gently pat your sea bass fillets completely dry with paper towels. A dry surface is essential for achieving that gorgeous crispy skin. Lay them in a baking dish or on a plate. Drizzle 1 tablespoon of olive oil over each fillet and use your hands to gently massage it, ensuring the fish is evenly coated. Now, season both sides of each fillet generously with the 1 teaspoon of kosher salt and a few good turns of freshly ground black pepper.
  3. Sear the Sea Bass (Skin-Side Down!): In your large sauté pan, add the remaining ¼ cup of olive oil. Place it over medium-high heat and let the oil heat for 2 to 3 minutes, until it shimmers, slides easily in the pan, and you might see just a wisp of smoke around the edges. This tells you the pan is perfectly hot for searing. Have a pot lid that fits inside your pan ready.
  4. Crisp the Skin: Reduce the heat to medium. Carefully lay one sea bass fillet, skin-side down, into the hot pan. Immediately place the pot lid on top of that fillet and gently press down for about 10 seconds. This ensures maximum contact between the fish skin and the hot pan, which is the secret to irresistibly crispy skin. Remove the pressure. If the edges of the fish start to curl up, re-apply the pressure for another 10 seconds. Repeat this process with the second fish fillet.
  5. Continue Cooking Fish on Stovetop: Continue to cook the fish, skin-side down, for 10 to 12 minutes. You're looking for that skin to turn a beautiful golden brown and become wonderfully crispy. Turn off the heat under the pan once the skin is perfectly crisp.
  6. Start the Pasta Stew: While your fish is searing, pour 3 cups of your prepared fish stock into your chosen 12-inch stovetop-to-oven cooking vessel (a cast-iron skillet or a wide, shallow pot works beautifully here). Bring the stock to a boil over high heat. Keep any remaining fish stock for another delicious use!
  7. Add Pasta and Tomatoes: Once the stock is boiling, add the mixed pastas. Cook them in the boiling stock for 4 minutes. This brief cooking time is just to soften the pasta slightly, as it will finish cooking in the oven. Add the cherry tomatoes to the pot.
  8. Flavor the Broth: Squeeze the juice from half a lemon directly into the simmering broth. Now, drizzle in the 1 tablespoon of colatura di alici. Give it a good stir to combine all those wonderful flavors. Colatura, a sort of Italian fish sauce, will add an incredible depth and umami to your stew.
  9. Combine Fish and Stew: Carefully lift the seared sea bass fillets from the sauté pan, taking care not to tear that beautiful crispy skin. Gently place them, skin-side up, into the pan with the simmering stock and pasta.
  10. Bake the Mischiato: Transfer the stovetop-to-oven cooking vessel, uncovered, to your preheated 375°F oven. Bake for 8 to 10 minutes, or until the largest pasta shapes are al dente—meaning they're cooked through but still have a slight bite to them. This is where the beauty of "mischiato potente" truly shines, with varying textures of pasta.
  11. Finish the Fennel Salad: While the mischiato bakes, uncover your reserved fennel slices. Squeeze the juice from the remaining half lemon over them and sprinkle with ½ teaspoon of kosher salt. Toss gently to coat. Drizzle with the 1 tablespoon of finishing-quality olive oil and sprinkle with the 1 tablespoon of finely chopped fresh Italian parsley. Toss again to combine. The acidity of the lemon and the salt will subtly "cook" the fennel, softening it just enough and bringing out its fresh, anise-like flavor.

IV. Serving:

  1. Remove from Oven: Carefully remove the mischiato from the oven.
  2. Assemble and Serve: Gently drop tangles of the vibrant fennel salad on top of the sea bass fillets, dividing it evenly among the portions. Sprinkle the remaining 2 tablespoons of finely chopped fresh Italian parsley over the mischiato, and finish with a generous drizzle of the remaining 2 tablespoons of finishing-quality extra-virgin olive oil. Serve immediately, letting everyone appreciate the beautiful fish and the rich, comforting stew.

Macronutrients per serving (estimated):

  • Calories: Approximately 600-700 kcal
  • Protein: Approximately 40-50g
  • Fat: Approximately 35-45g
  • Carbohydrates: Approximately 35-45g

Shopping List

Here's your shopping list for Mischiato Potente, organized to make your trip to the grocery store a breeze!

Produce Section:

  • 1 large fennel bulb (preferably with green tops)
  • 3 lemons (you'll need 2 for the recipe, plus an extra for the stock and finishing touches)
  • 1 large yellow onion
  • 1 large carrot
  • 1 celery stalk
  • 10-12 large garlic cloves (it's always good to have a few extra!)
  • 2 large fresh tomatoes (or 1 cup small red cherry tomatoes if using fresh for the mischiato)
  • 1 bunch fresh Italian parsley
  • 1 bunch fresh oregano
  • 1 bunch fresh tarragon
  • 1 bunch fresh rosemary

Seafood Counter:

  • One (2-pound) whole sea bass (or striped bass, black bass, or branzino), filleted, skin on (ask your fishmonger to reserve the head and bones – you'll need 2 pounds total for the stock!)

Pantry/Dry Goods:

  • ¼ pound mixed pastas (such as penne, trofie, orecchiette, fusilli, and spaghetti, or a pre-packaged mix of pasta shapes like "Mischiato Potente" if available)
  • Double-concentrated tomato paste (a small tube or can)
  • Dry white wine (something you'd enjoy drinking, like a Sauvignon Blanc or Pinot Grigio)
  • Kosher salt
  • Fresh coarsely ground black pepper

Oils & Vinegars:

  • Good quality olive oil (for cooking)
  • Finishing-quality extra-virgin olive oil (for drizzling)
  • Colatura di Alici (Italian fish sauce - look in the international aisle or specialty food section, or order online)

Canned Goods (Optional, if using instead of fresh):

  • 2 cups canned tomatoes (whole peeled or crushed, if not using fresh for stock)
  • 1 cup jarred Italian cherry tomatoes (datterini, Corbari, or pomodorini, if not using fresh for mischiato)

Happy Cooking!
